## 1. FULL WEBSITE AUDIT — NEW NEXT.JS SITE

### ABOVE THE FOLD — First Impression

**What's there:** A full-bleed hero image of a composite deck with pergola, "BUILDING MEMORIES TOGETHER" tagline, headline "Northern Virginia's Premium Deck Builder for Custom Outdoor Living," and a single "Discover More" button.

**What's wrong:**

The hero is aesthetically beautiful but conversion-dead. Here's why:

**"Building Memories Together"** is emotional fluff that says nothing to a Google Ads visitor who just searched "deck builder near me." They want to know: Can you build my deck? Are you available? How fast? That tagline wastes the most valuable real estate on the page.

**"Discover More"** is the weakest possible CTA for a lead-gen business. Nobody searching for a deck builder wants to "discover." They want to "Get a Free Quote," "Schedule My Consultation," or "Call Now." "Discover More" is an exploration CTA — appropriate for a museum, not a contractor trying to generate $20K leads.

**No phone number visible in the hero.** The "Call Now" button is in the nav, but Google Ads visitors on mobile need a giant, tappable phone number INSIDE the hero, not hidden in navigation.

**No form visible above the fold.** The contact form is buried at the very bottom of the page. A visitor from a Google Ad has to scroll through the ENTIRE page to find a way to submit their information. This is a massive conversion killer.

**No social proof in the hero.** No "5.0 Stars on Google" badge, no "41 Reviews," no "100+ Decks Built." The old WordPress site at least had trust badges immediately below the fold.

### TRUST SIGNALS

The site shows 4 trust badges below the hero: 2 Years Labor Warranty, Sustainable Materials, Licensed Company, Free Estimates. These are generic and weak. "2 Years Labor Warranty" actually undermines confidence — competitors tout 5-year or lifetime warranties. "Sustainable Materials" is vague. "Licensed Company" should be the bare minimum, not a selling point. "Free Estimates" is expected by everyone.

The BBB, Thumbtack, Hotfrog, and Brownbook logos are near the bottom — they need to be visible much higher up and accompanied by your Google review count/rating.

### VISUAL HIERARCHY

The page flows: Hero → Trust Badges → About Section → Services Cards → Services Detail → Testimonials → FAQ → Third-Party Logos → Contact Form + Footer. This is backwards for lead generation. The contact form and phone number should be accessible within the first scroll, not after 8+ scroll-lengths.

### CTA PLACEMENT & STRENGTH

There are only three CTAs on the entire homepage: "Discover More" (hero), "Discover More" (about section), and "More Services." None of them say "Get a Free Quote" or "Call Now" until the very bottom. The entire mid-section is a CTA desert. A high-ticket lead gen page needs a CTA visible at every scroll-depth.

### TESTIMONIALS

Three testimonials from "Sarah Jenkins," "Michael Thompson," and "Robert & Linda" with generic titles "Satisfied Customer." These feel fabricated. No last names with verifiable detail, no Google review links, no city/state. Your old site mentions actual Google reviews. Competitors like xScape have 100+ Google reviews with full names and locations. This is a huge credibility gap.

### PSYCHOLOGICAL TRIGGERS — What's Missing

No urgency (seasonal booking, limited availability). No specificity (number of projects completed, years in business, specific materials used). No risk reversal (money-back guarantee language, satisfaction guarantee). No authority (certifications, manufacturer partnerships — your old site showed Trex, TimberTech, Fiberon logos). No scarcity (booking calendar filling up).

### What IS Strong

The photography on the hero is high-quality and professional. The site loads fast on Next.js (good perceived performance). The orange brand color is distinctive. The services page is well-organized with clear categories. The showcase portfolio has real project photos with locations and dates.

## 3. GOOGLE ADS LANDING PAGE ANALYSIS

### Does it match search intent?

When someone searches "deck builder near me" or "composite deck builder Northern Virginia" and clicks your ad, they land on a page that says "Building Memories Together" and "Discover More." This is a complete intent mismatch. The searcher wants to find a contractor, see proof of quality, and submit their info FAST. Your page asks them to go on a journey.

### Does it filter low-quality leads?

No. The messaging is completely generic. There's no price anchoring, no mention of project minimums, nothing that says "Our typical projects start at $15,000." The phrase "At Prices That Fit Your Budget" actively ATTRACTS budget shoppers. The inclusion of "Deck Washing" as a homepage service draws in small-ticket cleaning jobs when you want full builds.

### Does it attract high-ticket clients?

Partially. The word "Premium" in the hero helps. But there's no portfolio showcase visible above the fold showing luxury builds. No project value ranges. No material-specific language (Trex, TimberTech) that signals premium. No financing options mentioned that signal bigger projects.

### Is it too "pretty" vs "conversion-focused"?

Yes. This site looks like it was designed by a designer, not a conversion strategist. Beautiful typography, generous whitespace, subtle animations — but zero urgency, zero friction-reducing elements, zero conversion triggers. It's a portfolio website, not a lead generation machine.

### Why Current Structure Increases CPA

Your ad budget is sending traffic to a page that: makes visitors scroll 8+ times to find a form; uses a passive CTA that doesn't compel action; shows no social proof above the fold; includes low-ticket services that attract wrong leads; has no phone number visible in the hero section; offers no urgency or scarcity messaging.

Every one of these issues causes drop-off. If even 20% of ad visitors bounce because they can't find the form fast enough, your CPA increases by 25%.

### Where Users Drop Off

The biggest drop-off zone is immediately after the hero. The visitor sees "Discover More," doesn't feel compelled to click, and bounces. The second drop-off zone is the long "Our Introduction" section — it's about YOU, not about THEM. The third is the FAQ section, which feels like the page is ending without ever asking the visitor to take action.

## 4. LEAD QUALITY FILTER SYSTEM

### Messaging Filters

Your copy needs to signal premium pricing without being off-putting. Replace "At Prices That Fit Your Budget" (attracts bargain hunters) with language like "Custom-Designed Outdoor Living Spaces Starting at $15K" or "Premium Composite Decks Engineered for Northern Virginia Homes." The word "budget" should never appear on your site.

Remove or de-emphasize "Deck Washing" from the homepage. That's a $200-500 job. Keep it on a services sub-page for SEO if needed, but it shouldn't be one of the first four services a visitor sees. Lead with New Decks, Deck Replacement/Resurfacing, Screened Porches, and Premium Outdoor Living.

### CTA Filters

Instead of "Get Free Estimate," consider "Request Your Design Consultation." "Free Estimate" attracts price-shoppers. "Design Consultation" attracts people ready to invest in a planned project. Your form should ask for project type, approximate timeline, and budget range — this alone filters out 80% of unqualified leads.

### Form Structure (Recommended)

The bottom-of-page form currently has: Name, Email, Phone, Service Dropdown, Message. This captures everyone including tire-kickers. A filtering form would include:

- Name, Phone, Email (basics)
- What type of project? (dropdown: New Deck Build, Deck Replacement, Screened Porch, Other Outdoor Living)
- Approximate budget range (dropdown: $10K-$20K, $20K-$40K, $40K+, Not Sure)
- When are you looking to start? (dropdown: Within 1 month, 1-3 months, 3-6 months, Just exploring)
- Brief project description (textarea)

The budget and timeline dropdowns immediately filter leads. Anyone selecting "Just exploring" or who doesn't want to indicate budget self-selects out. The form heading should say "Request Your Free Design Consultation" not just "Request A Free Quote."

### Copy That Repels Low-Budget Clients

Throughout the site, use phrases that signal premium: "custom-engineered," "precision-built," "design-build process," "premium composite materials," "Trex Platinum installer." Avoid: "affordable," "budget-friendly," "cheap," "discount," "deals." The words "custom" and "premium" should be the two most-repeated adjectives on the entire site.

## 8. CRITICAL MISTAKES — BRUTAL HONESTY

**1. The homepage has no lead form above the fold.** This single issue could be responsible for 40-60% of lost conversions from paid traffic. Every competitor has a form or prominent phone number visible immediately.

**2. "Discover More" is your primary CTA.** This is a browsing CTA, not a conversion CTA. It sends people deeper into the site instead of converting them. Every click away from conversion is a lost opportunity.

**3. "At Prices That Fit Your Budget" actively attracts the wrong audience.** This phrase tells bargain hunters "we're cheap enough for you." It should be removed and replaced with premium-positioning language.

**4. Deck Washing is featured as a core homepage service.** This is a $200-500 job. You're spending Google Ads dollars to show this to people who came looking for a $20K deck. It dilutes your positioning and attracts low-ticket inquiries.

**5. The testimonials appear fabricated.** Generic names, no locations, no verification, stock-looking avatar photos. This actively hurts trust instead of building it. Real Google reviews with verifiable names and locations are non-negotiable.

**6. No manufacturer certifications visible.** Trex and TimberTech are the two most-searched composite deck brands. Your old site had these logos. The new site removed them. This is leaving money on the table for branded search queries.

**7. No "How It Works" process section.** High-ticket buyers have anxiety about the process. Not explaining your steps (consult → design → build → warranty) forces them to figure it out themselves — many won't bother and will go to a competitor who makes it clear.

**8. The form asks for too little information.** Name, Email, Phone, Service, Message — this captures everyone, including tire-kickers. Adding budget and timeline fields would filter leads dramatically and give your sales team better data.

**9. No chat widget.** The old site had Tidio chat. The new site removed it. Some visitors will never fill out a form or make a phone call — chat is their preferred contact method. You lost a lead channel.

**10. No sticky/persistent CTA.** Once a visitor scrolls past the header navigation, the only way to contact you is to scroll all the way back up or all the way to the bottom. There should be a floating "Call Now" or "Get Quote" button visible at all times.
